Only choose antibiotics as medicine if it is absolutely necessary. A healthy person should have only a few rounds of antibiotics in the course of a lifetime. Antibiotics kill both our good and bad bacteria, leaving us very much out of balance and susceptible for other pathogens to enter - creating a vicious cycle of antibiotic overuse.
It’s estimated that 20-50% of antibiotic use is inappropriate and that’s a conservative estimate.
If at all possible, explore alternatives to the following medications that are detrimental to your gut flora: birth control, acid suppressants (PPIs), antacids, steroids, horomonal replacement therapy or NSAIDs.
Do your best to avoid antibiotics in your food supply. If you eat meat, dairy, eggs, fish or shellfish, choose organic and grass fed or wild caught when possible.
Do your best to minimize chlorine exposure - whether drinking, showering or swimming (after all, you skin is your largest organ).
Did you know that 80% of the antibiotic use in the US is consumed by livestock?
Focus on a whole food diet and avoid processed and packaged foods. Try shopping on the outside aisles of the supermarket - where the fresh items reside. Avoid refined sugars, grains, oils and condiments. Eat lots of fresh fruits, vegetables, nuts and seeds (80% of your diet, ideally).
Spend time outside, in nature. Go for walks, move your body and center yourself. De-stress by finding your alignment and sense of well-being.
One study suggests that our microbiome is significantly affected by our diet and that any changes can take place relatively quickly.
Get your hands dirty if possible. Rather than obsess over antimicrobials, plant a garden or play with a dog. Rather than focus on trying to kill the bad bacteria, focus on repopulating yourself with the good bacteria - because the good guys will keep the bad guys in check for you!
Introduce prebiotic foods into your diet to help your probiotics colonize. Prebiotics are to probiotics what fertilizer is to a garden. They are the foods that nourish the healthy bacteria and help them to “set up shop’” and thrive in your GI tract. The best ones are raw chicory root, dandelion greens, garlic, leek and onion, and also bananas (all raw) and whole grains (steel-cut oats are good, and generally the more unrefined the better). By definition, prebiotics are non-digestible fiber compounds that pass undigested through the upper part of the gastrointestinal tract and stimulate the growth and/or activity of advantageous bacteria that colonize the large bowel by acting as substrate for them.
